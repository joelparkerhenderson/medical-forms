# OSCE Pre-operative Assessment Guide

Aligned with the Geeky Medics *Anaesthetic Pre-operative Assessment OSCE Guide*
and the CPOC *Preoperative Assessment and Optimisation for Adult Surgery*
(June 2021). Structured as a focused history, examination, and
investigations check-list that an anaesthetist, surgeon, or pre-op nurse
completes in clinic.

## Purpose

All patients having an operation under general, regional, or anaesthesia-led
sedation require a pre-operative assessment. Its aims are to:

1. Identify medical comorbidities that affect anaesthetic risk.
2. Optimise the patient's physiological state before surgery.
3. Identify patients who need enhanced post-operative care (HDU / ICU).
4. Support shared decision making and informed consent (Montgomery).
5. Produce an anaesthesia plan and WHO Safer Surgery Checklist entry.

Full medical clerking is not always necessary. The emphasis is on **airway,
cardiorespiratory, and functional-capacity** assessment.

## Introduction

- Introduce yourself (name, role).
- Confirm patient identity: name, DOB, NHS number.
- Explain purpose of the assessment and gain verbal consent.
- Involve carer / family member if the patient wishes.

## Focused history

### 1. Presenting complaint and proposed procedure

- Planned operation, indication, laterality.
- Surgeon, urgency (elective / urgent / emergency), planned anaesthesia.
- Estimated blood loss and duration.
- Patient's understanding of the operation (shared decision making).

### 2. Past medical history

Ask system-by-system, targeting conditions known to affect anaesthesia.

- **Cardiac:** ischaemic heart disease, angina (CCS class), previous MI,
  arrhythmia (AF, heart block), heart failure (NYHA class), valvular disease,
  pacemaker / ICD, hypertension, recent cardiac intervention (PCI, CABG).
- **Respiratory:** asthma (control, rescue inhaler use), COPD, obstructive
  sleep apnoea, bronchiectasis, pulmonary hypertension, smoking (pack-years).
- **Neurological:** previous stroke / TIA, epilepsy, Parkinson disease,
  multiple sclerosis, myasthenia gravis, peripheral neuropathy, cognitive
  impairment, chronic pain.
- **Endocrine:** diabetes (type, control, complications), thyroid disease,
  adrenal insufficiency, steroid use (dose and duration).
- **Renal:** chronic kidney disease, dialysis, fluid balance issues.
- **Hepatic:** chronic liver disease, hepatitis, jaundice.
- **Haematological:** anaemia, bleeding disorders, thrombophilia, recent
  transfusion.
- **Gastrointestinal:** reflux, hiatus hernia, previous gastric surgery.
- **Musculoskeletal:** rheumatoid arthritis (cervical-spine involvement),
  limited joint ROM relevant to positioning or regional anaesthesia.
- **Other:** recent COVID-19 infection (timing, severity, long-COVID), recent
  hospital admissions, recent new diagnoses.

### 3. Previous anaesthesia and surgery

- Previous operations and anaesthetic techniques used.
- Any anaesthetic complications: difficult intubation, awareness, post-op
  nausea and vomiting, malignant hyperthermia, suxamethonium apnoea,
  pseudocholinesterase deficiency.
- Family history of anaesthetic problems.

### 4. Medications

- Full reconciled list including dose, frequency, route, last dose.
- Particular attention to:
  - **Anticoagulants** (warfarin, DOACs, heparins, aspirin, clopidogrel).
  - **Antihypertensives** (ACE-inhibitors, ARBs, beta-blockers, diuretics).
  - **Diabetes** (insulin, sulfonylureas, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists).
  - **Steroids** (may need perioperative hydrocortisone cover).
  - **Opioids and other dependency-forming drugs** (DFDs).
  - **Herbal remedies and over-the-counter** preparations.
- Consult the UKCPA *Handbook of Perioperative Medicines* for hold / continue
  decisions.

### 5. Allergies

- Drugs (especially antibiotics, NSAIDs, neuromuscular blocking agents).
- Latex.
- Foods (may cross-react with anaesthetic agents, e.g. egg / soya and
  propofol).
- Adhesive and contact allergies.
- Nature of reaction: anaphylaxis vs rash vs GI upset.

### 6. Social history

- Smoking: current, pack-years, cessation attempts.
- Alcohol: units per week, dependency, withdrawal risk.
- Recreational drugs.
- Occupation (e.g. professional singer — airway management implications).
- Social support at home (relevant to discharge planning and day-case
  suitability).
- Safeguarding concerns.

### 7. Functional capacity

Use an objective tool:

- **Duke Activity Status Index (DASI)** questionnaire.
- **METs estimate** (< 4 METs = poor; ≥ 4 = adequate for most surgery).
- Ability to climb two flights of stairs, walk briskly, do heavy housework.
- Cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET) if available for high-risk patients.

### 8. Fasting status (day of surgery)

- Solids: 6 hours.
- Clear fluids: 2 hours (sips of water up to induction under current
  enhanced recovery guidance).
- Breast milk: 4 hours; infant formula: 6 hours.

## Examination

### 9. General inspection

- Pallor, jaundice, cyanosis, clubbing, oedema, cachexia.
- Hydration status.
- Ability to mobilise, transfer.

### 10. Airway

This is the most important component for the anaesthetist.

- **Mallampati class (I–IV)**: patient sits upright, opens mouth maximally,
  protrudes tongue.
  - I: soft palate, uvula, fauces, pillars visible.
  - II: soft palate, uvula, fauces visible.
  - III: soft palate and base of uvula visible.
  - IV: hard palate only visible.
- **Thyromental distance** (chin to thyroid notch with neck extended): < 6 cm
  predicts difficult intubation.
- **Mouth opening / inter-incisor gap**: < 3 cm is abnormal.
- **Neck range of motion**: flexion and extension.
- **Cervical spine stability**: rheumatoid arthritis, trauma, ankylosing
  spondylitis.
- **Dentition**: loose teeth, caps, crowns, dentures, edentulous.
- **Beard**: may complicate mask ventilation.
- **Jaw protrusion**: upper-lip bite test (ability to cover upper lip with
  lower incisors).
- **Prior difficult intubation** documented in anaesthetic record.

### 11. Cardiovascular

- Pulse: rate, rhythm, character.
- Blood pressure (both arms if indicated).
- JVP.
- Apex beat, thrills, heaves.
- Heart sounds: added sounds, murmurs (timing, character, radiation).
- Peripheral oedema, peripheral pulses.
- Carotid bruits.

### 12. Respiratory

- Respiratory rate, SpO₂ on room air.
- Chest wall: deformity, scars, expansion.
- Percussion.
- Auscultation: breath sounds, added sounds (wheeze, crackles, crepitations,
  pleural rub).
- Peak expiratory flow if asthma / COPD.

### 13. Abdomen

- Scars, distension, organomegaly, reflux assessment (consider rapid-sequence
  induction if significant GORD or hiatus hernia).
- Fasting status check.

### 14. Neurological

- GCS.
- Cognition: AMT-4 (for delirium screening) or MOCA if concerns.
- Cranial nerves summary.
- Motor power, tone, reflexes.
- Sensory.
- Gait if mobility-limited.

### 15. Musculoskeletal & integumentary

- Spine for neuraxial anaesthesia (scoliosis, previous surgery, tattoos).
- Joint ROM for positioning (hips, shoulders, neck).
- Skin condition at IV access and regional block sites.

## Investigations

Order investigations per **NICE NG45** and local anaesthetist judgement. See
also `nice-ng45-tests.md`.

### 16. Standard pre-op panel (as indicated)

- **FBC** — ASA 3+ or major surgery, suspected anaemia / bleeding disorder.
- **U&Es / creatinine / eGFR** — ASA 3+, major surgery, or nephrotoxic
  medication.
- **LFTs** — liver disease, alcohol excess, hepatotoxic drugs.
- **Coagulation screen (INR, APTT)** — anticoagulants, liver disease,
  neuraxial anaesthesia.
- **Group & Save / Crossmatch** — high-blood-loss surgery per local policy.
- **Glucose / HbA1c** — diabetes, unexplained weight loss.
- **TSH** — thyroid disease.
- **Pregnancy test** — women of reproductive age before general anaesthesia.
- **Sickle-cell screen** — high-risk ethnic groups without prior testing.

### 17. Imaging / dynamic tests

- **ECG** — age ≥ 65, cardiovascular disease, major surgery.
- **Chest X-ray** — respiratory symptoms, suspected pathology (not routine).
- **Echocardiogram** — heart failure, new murmur, significant valve disease.
- **Pulmonary function tests** — COPD, asthma with poor control, thoracic
  surgery.
- **CPET** — high-risk patients undergoing major surgery.

## Scoring and risk stratification

- **ASA Physical Status** (I–VI) — see `asa-grading-rules.md`.
- **Mallampati class** (I–IV).
- **Revised Cardiac Risk Index (RCRI)** — 6 predictors, score 0–6.
- **STOP-BANG** (0–8) for obstructive sleep apnoea.
- **Clinical Frailty Scale** (1–9) for patients ≥ 65.
- **DASI / METs** for functional capacity.
- **Surgical Outcome Risk Tool (SORT)** for 30-day mortality estimate.

## Anaesthesia plan

Produced by the anaesthetist on step 15.

- Technique: GA / regional / sedation / MAC / combined.
- Airway plan: supraglottic device / ETT / awake fibreoptic intubation.
- Monitoring: standard AAGBI / invasive / cardiac output.
- Analgesia: regional block, multimodal opioid-sparing, PCA.
- DVT prophylaxis: mechanical ± pharmacological.
- Post-op disposition: ward / enhanced care / HDU / ICU.
- Anticipated length of stay.
- Special equipment: difficult-airway trolley, cell-saver, ultrasound.

## Communication and consent

- Discuss risks using the BRAN framework (Benefits, Risks, Alternatives,
  doing Nothing).
- Use natural-frequency risk communication (e.g. "1 in 100" rather than "1
  percent").
- Document shared-decision-making conversation and patient preferences.
- Signed consent form per Trust policy.

## Sign-off

- Electronic signature of the clinician who completed the assessment.
- GMC / NMC / HCPC registration number recorded.
- Timestamp.
- Attach to patient record and WHO Safer Surgery Checklist.
